Why InMail ends up costing more than it looks

The $99/month headline price assumes perfect credit utilization. You get 50 credits per month. If you send 50 InMails every month with zero wasted messages, you're at $1.98 per contact. That's not how it goes for most people.

LinkedIn's own engagement data suggests most Sales Navigator users send 15-30 InMails per month, not 50. The rest go unused. Unused credits roll over for 90 days, then expire. So your effective cost per InMail sent is typically $3-7 before you factor in the time spent writing each message manually.

There's also a structural ceiling: you can't scale InMail without buying more credits. Cold email has no such ceiling. You set a budget, and the volume scales with it.

The open rate argument for InMail

The usual defense for InMail's higher cost is that it gets better open rates. Here's what that claim actually holds up to.

01
LinkedIn's claimed InMail open rates: 18-35% LinkedIn reports that InMail gets 18-35% open rates, higher than "email." But this stat compares against all email, including marketing newsletters and transactional messages. Cold email targeting is much more precise, which changes the comparison.
02
Targeted cold email open rates: 27% average Targeted cold email campaigns with personalized subject lines average 27% open rates. Well-crafted campaigns with strong ICP targeting regularly hit 40-50%. The gap between InMail and cold email, on open rates, is narrower than LinkedIn suggests.
03
What actually matters is cost per reply At $3 per InMail with a 5% reply rate, you're paying $60 per reply. At $0.07 per cold email with a 4% reply rate, you're paying $1.75 per reply. Even if InMail outperforms cold email on reply rate by 2x, cold email still wins on cost per conversation by a wide margin.
04
InMail has real advantages in one specific case For reaching senior executives who don't have their work email listed anywhere, InMail is often the only option. LinkedIn is also more appropriate for relationship-nurturing messages than cold outreach. If you know the person or have a warm connection, InMail is the right tool. For mass prospecting, the economics don't work.

Common questions

Can I get InMail credits cheaper than $99/month?

LinkedIn's InMail credits are only available through a Sales Navigator or Recruiter subscription. Sales Navigator starts at $99/month billed monthly, or $79.99/month billed annually. There's no way to buy InMail credits standalone. The only discount path is the annual plan, which saves about $19/month per seat.

Do LinkedIn InMail credits roll over?

Unused InMail credits roll over for up to 90 days. After that, they expire. LinkedIn added this to reduce waste, but in practice it means you can stockpile 3 months of credits before they start expiring. If you're not sending 50 InMails per month consistently, you're still not getting full value from the subscription.

Does LinkedIn penalize you for sending too many InMails?

Yes. LinkedIn tracks InMail acceptance rates. If your InMails are accepted at below 13% (LinkedIn's threshold), the platform reduces or suspends your InMail credits. This means writing low-quality, mass InMails hurts your ability to send any InMails at all. It's a feedback loop that penalizes lazy personalization.

What's a realistic reply rate for cold email vs InMail?

Cold email with targeted lists and personalized copy averages 3-8% reply rates. InMail reply rates vary widely: LinkedIn claims up to 25% for well-crafted messages, but industry benchmarks put the average closer to 5-15%. The biggest variable in both channels is how well you've identified who actually has the problem you solve.

Should I use both InMail and cold email together?

For high-value targets where deal size justifies the InMail cost, a multichannel approach works. Send a cold email first, then follow up with an InMail 3-5 days later if no reply. The second touchpoint on a different channel gets noticeably better response rates than either channel alone. Reserve this for accounts where the potential deal size makes the extra $3-5 worth it.
