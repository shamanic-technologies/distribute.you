Cold email cost per contact: the real numbers in 2026

Most cost comparisons stop at the tool subscription. They skip the data provider, the domain setup, the inbox warmup, and the hours you spend maintaining it. Here's the full cost, broken down component by component.

What "cost per contact" actually includes

To reach one verified prospect with one personalized cold email, you need four things. Most pricing discussions only show you one of them.

01
Prospect data (finding and verifying the email) Apollo costs $49/month for 1,000 email credits on the Basic plan. That's $0.049 per contact before you validate. Email validation with ZeroBounce or NeverBounce adds another $0.003-0.008 per email. Total data cost: roughly $0.05-0.06 per contact at this tier.
02
Sending infrastructure (domains and inbox warmup) Sending cold email from your main domain risks damaging its deliverability. Standard practice is 2-3 separate sending domains at $12-15/year each, plus 2-3 email inboxes per domain (typically Google Workspace at $6/month per inbox). Warmup takes 4-6 weeks, during which you can't send at scale. Amortized infrastructure cost: $0.03-0.08 per contact depending on volume.
03
Sending tool (sequencing, tracking, follow-up) Instantly's basic plan is $37/month for unlimited emails. Lemlist starts at $59/month. At 1,000 contacts per month, that's $0.037-0.059 per contact for the sending layer. At 500 contacts per month, costs double because the fixed monthly fee is spread thinner.
04
Copy (research and writing per contact) Generic templates pull open rates down to 10-15%. Personalized emails need research: reading the prospect's website, finding a relevant hook, writing something specific. At 30 minutes per 50 personalized emails (fast), your time cost at $50/hour is $0.50 per contact just for writing. Most founders undercount this.

Why DIY is cheaper than agencies but not as cheap as it looks

The $126/month tool stack sounds like $0.13 per contact at 1,000 sends. But that math assumes maximum utilization. In practice, most founders are sending 200-400 contacts per month on a DIY setup, which puts the fixed cost at $0.32-0.63 per contact for tools alone.

Then there's the time. Writing 400 personalized cold emails per month takes 4-6 hours, minimum. At founder hourly rates, that's $200-500 of opportunity cost that never shows up in the tool bill. The "cheap" option stops looking cheap fast.

Agencies eliminate the time problem but add back the cost. $5,000/month gets you 500-1,000 contacts reached at $5-10 per contact. Whether that math works depends on your average contract value. If you close 1 deal per 100 contacts and your average deal is $5,000, you break even. If your average deal is $500, it doesn't work.

How to calculate your actual cost per contact

A quick formula that works for any setup you're considering.

01
Add up your fixed monthly costs Data tool + sending tool + infrastructure (domains, inboxes, warmup service). For a typical DIY stack: Apollo $49 + Instantly $37 + 2 Google Workspace inboxes $12 + 2 domains $2 = $100/month fixed.
02
Divide by monthly contacts sent If you send 500 contacts per month, your fixed tool cost is $0.20 per contact. At 1,000 contacts, it's $0.10. This is why volume matters for DIY: the math only works when you're using the tools at near-capacity.
03
Add your time cost Estimate hours spent weekly on cold email (writing, list management, deliverability monitoring, reply routing). Multiply by your hourly rate or the hourly cost of whoever does it. Divide by monthly sends. For most solo founders, this adds $0.30-0.80 per contact to the tool cost.
04
Compare to cost per qualified reply The more useful number is cost per reply: total spend divided by positive replies. At $0.07/contact with a 4% reply rate, that's $1.75 per reply. At $0.40/contact (DIY) with the same reply rate, it's $10 per reply. The reply rate often improves with personalization, which changes both sides of the fraction.

Common questions about cold email cost per contact

What's a good cost per qualified reply for cold email?

For B2B SaaS, under $5 per qualified reply is strong. Under $10 is solid. Above $20, the channel needs work (targeting or copy). At Distribute's $0.07/contact with a 4% positive reply rate, you're at $1.75 per qualified reply. Compare that to LinkedIn ads at $80-300 per lead or a paid search CPL of $40-200 for B2B.

Why does Apollo charge per email credit instead of per contact?

Apollo separates the cost of viewing a contact's full profile from the cost of accessing their email address. Basic contact views are unlimited on most plans. Email credits unlock verified email addresses. On the Basic plan at $49/month, you get 1,000 email credits. Once those are used, additional credits cost $0.05 each. At scale, the cost per email can be lower if you're on a higher tier, but the unit economics change per plan.

Can I get cold email cost per contact below $0.10 with a DIY stack?

On tool costs alone: yes, at high volume. At 5,000+ contacts per month with Apollo Pro ($99/month), Instantly Growth ($97/month), and solid infrastructure, tool cost per contact drops below $0.05. But this assumes full utilization and excludes your time. It also requires managing deliverability, list quality, and sequences yourself. Most founders don't reach that volume or sustain that maintenance load.

Does email verification cost count separately?

On a DIY stack, yes. ZeroBounce and NeverBounce charge $0.003-0.008 per verification. If you're validating a list of 1,000 contacts, that's $3-8, which sounds minor but adds up. It's also a step many beginners skip, which leads to bounce rates above 5% and deliverability problems. Distribute handles verification internally before sending, so it's included in the $0.07.

How does Distribute keep cost per contact so low?

Scale and automation. Distribute runs shared infrastructure across many campaigns simultaneously, so the fixed costs are amortized across a large volume of sends. The AI research and copy generation costs a fraction of what a human writer would charge per contact. The result is a cost structure that's closer to what a large SDR team at a big company achieves than what a solo founder can replicate with a DIY stack.
