LinkedIn InMail cost vs cold email: what the math actually says

Sales Navigator gives you 50 InMail credits for $99 a month. That's $1.98 per message on paper. Real-world cost, once you count unused credits, lands at $3-5. Cold email runs $0.07. Here's why the gap is that wide.

The InMail credit math

LinkedIn buries the per-message cost inside subscription tiers, 90-day rollover rules, and credit pack upsells. Here's the calculation that most comparisons skip.

Sales Navigator Core costs $99/month billed monthly, or $79.99/month on an annual plan. Each seat includes 50 InMail credits. At perfect utilization, that's $1.98 per message. Two problems with that number:

  • Most users don't send 50 InMails a month. Typical utilization runs 15-30 credits per month.
  • Unused credits roll over for 90 days, then expire. You can't carry them indefinitely.

At 20 InMails per month on a $99 plan, your effective cost is $4.95 per message before you write a single word. If you run out and buy credit packs, LinkedIn charges $10 for 10 additional credits, on top of the subscription.

Real-world cost per message

The $1.98 headline only works if you're a dedicated InMail sender who religiously hits 50 per month. For most people using Sales Navigator for search, account tracking, and the occasional message, InMail is a secondary feature with a per-message cost that runs much higher.

There's also a volume ceiling. Cold email has no platform cap. You can reach 500 contacts this week by increasing your budget. With InMail, you're capped at 50 unless you buy more credits. That ceiling matters for any founder who wants to run a real outbound motion.

How DIY cold email compares: Apollo ($49/month) + Instantly ($37/month) + infrastructure ($40/month) = $126 fixed cost. At 500 contacts/month, that's $0.25 per contact for tools, before your time writing and maintaining it. Distribute at $0.07 all-in is cheaper even than a well-run DIY stack at moderate volume.

The open rate argument for InMail

The usual defense for InMail's higher cost is that it gets better engagement. LinkedIn claims 18-35% open rates for InMail, which they compare against "email" broadly. That comparison is doing a lot of work.

"Email" in that stat includes marketing newsletters, transactional confirmations, and promotional blasts. Those categories drag averages down. Targeted cold email with a specific ICP and personalized subject lines is a different category. Campaigns with tight targeting regularly hit 27-45% open rates.

The gap between InMail and targeted cold email on open rates is narrower than LinkedIn's marketing suggests. Whether InMail's engagement edge, if it exists, is worth 43x the cost per contact is a math question with a pretty clear answer.

Cost per reply: the number that matters

Open rate is a vanity metric if you're not tracking what happens next. Cost per qualified reply is the number that connects to pipeline. Here's how it breaks down across channels:

  • InMail at $4 per message, 10% reply rate: $40 per reply
  • InMail at $4 per message, 15% reply rate: $26.67 per reply
  • Cold email at $0.07, 4% reply rate: $1.75 per reply
  • Cold email at $0.07, 8% reply rate: $0.88 per reply

For InMail to match cold email's cost per reply at $1.75, it would need a reply rate above 228%. That's not a realistic scenario. Even if InMail genuinely outperforms cold email on reply rates by 3x, cold email still wins on cost per conversation by a factor of 15-20x.

The math only closes for InMail in one case: when deal size is large enough that cost per reply doesn't matter much, and when the specific prospect is unreachable any other way.

When InMail actually makes sense

InMail isn't a bad tool. It's the wrong tool for volume prospecting. Three cases where it makes sense:

Senior executives with no listed email: Some C-suite contacts don't have their work email listed anywhere. LinkedIn is often the only way to reach them with a direct message. At $4-5 per message for a target who's worth $50,000+ in deal value, the cost is irrelevant.

Warm follow-up after a trigger: Someone viewed your LinkedIn profile or engaged with a post. An InMail arriving the same day has a much higher reply rate than a cold one. This isn't mass outreach, it's a manual signal-based touchpoint.

Multichannel sequencing for high-value accounts: Send cold email first. If no reply after 4-5 days, follow up with an InMail. The second channel touchpoint boosts response rates noticeably. Reserve the InMail credit for prospects where the deal size justifies the extra $4-5.

For general outbound prospecting, the economics don't work at $3-5 per message versus $0.07.
