Cold email cost per contact: the real numbers in 2026

Most cold email cost comparisons quote the tool subscription. They skip the data provider, the domain setup, the inbox warmup, and the hours you spend maintaining it. Here's the full stack, broken down component by component.

The four components of cost per contact

To reach one verified prospect with one personalized cold email, you need four things. Most pricing discussions only show you one.

1. Prospect data. Finding verified email addresses costs money whether you use Apollo ($49/month for 1,000 credits on the Basic plan), ZoomInfo, or Clay. Add email validation via ZeroBounce or NeverBounce at $0.003-0.008 per address and you're spending roughly $0.05-0.06 per contact on data alone.

2. Sending infrastructure. Sending cold email from your main domain risks damaging its deliverability. Standard practice is 2-3 separate sending domains at $12-15/year each, plus Google Workspace inboxes at $6/month per inbox. New domains need 4-6 weeks of warmup before you can send at scale. Amortized across your volume, infrastructure adds $0.03-0.08 per contact depending on how many contacts you're sending to each month.

3. Sending tool. Instantly ($37/month), Lemlist ($59/month), or Smartlead ($39/month) handle sequencing, tracking, and follow-ups. At 1,000 contacts per month, tool cost runs $0.04-0.06 per contact. At 500 contacts per month, it doubles because fixed costs spread thinner.

4. Copy and research. Generic templates pull open rates to 10-15%. Personalized emails require research: reading the prospect's website, finding a relevant hook, writing something specific. At 30 minutes per 50 personalized emails, your time cost at $50/hour is $0.50 per contact just for writing. Most founders undercount this completely.

The number most quotes are missing: When someone says "cold email costs $0.15 per contact," they usually mean tools only. Add data and time and you're typically at $0.40-0.80 for a real DIY operation. That's still much cheaper than agencies at $2-15, but it's not $0.15.

DIY stack: the actual math

Here's a common setup: Apollo Basic ($49) + Instantly Starter ($37) + 2 Google Workspace inboxes ($12) + 2 sending domains ($2/month amortized) = $100/month fixed cost.

At different send volumes, here's what that fixed cost translates to per contact:

  • 200 contacts/month: $0.50 per contact (tools only)
  • 500 contacts/month: $0.20 per contact (tools only)
  • 1,000 contacts/month: $0.10 per contact (tools only)
  • 2,000 contacts/month: $0.05 per contact (tools only)

The math only works at volume. A founder sending 200 contacts a month is paying $0.50 per contact in tools before adding data costs or writing time. At that scale, DIY isn't cheap. It just feels cheap because the $100/month subscription line item looks small.

$0.10 tool-only cost per contact on a $100/month DIY stack at 1,000 sends/month. Add data ($0.05) and writing time ($0.30+) and you're at $0.45 per contact total.

Agency cost breakdown

Cold email agencies typically charge $3,000-$10,000/month on retainer. What that gets you: 500-2,000 contacts prospected per month, copy written and tested, infrastructure managed, and replies filtered. At median, that's $2-15 per contact reached.

The agency cost looks high per contact until you compare it to an in-house SDR. A junior SDR in North America costs $60,000-$90,000 loaded (salary + benefits + tools + management time). At 50-100 manual emails per day, they reach 1,000-2,000 contacts per month, which puts cost per contact at $5-7.50.

Both options are in the same range. Both require a minimum 2-4 week ramp before you see results. Both require your time for briefing, copy review, and ongoing adjustments. Neither scales without adding headcount or budget.

The hidden time cost

The numbers above assume you're good at cold email and your setup works. Getting there takes time that never shows up on a tool invoice.

Domain warmup: 4-6 weeks of automated sending before you can run real campaigns. During this time, you're paying for infrastructure that isn't producing results. Initial setup: importing contacts, writing copy, setting up sequences, and configuring deliverability settings takes 8-15 hours for a first-time user. Ongoing maintenance: monitoring bounce rates, updating copy when reply rates drop, replacing contacts in sequences, and handling domain reputation issues runs 3-5 hours per week.

That's roughly 20-25 hours in the first month and 12-20 hours per month ongoing. At a $75/hour opportunity cost, you're adding $900-1,500/month in time cost that the tool subscription never captures.

Whether that's worth it depends on whether cold email becomes a repeatable channel for your business. If you're validating whether outbound works at all, the 6-8 week DIY experiment to find out is genuinely expensive in time. How to calculate your actual cost

A simple formula that works for any setup:

Tool cost per contact = (monthly tool subscriptions + infrastructure) / contacts sent per month

Data cost per contact = data provider monthly cost / contacts sourced per month

Time cost per contact = (weekly hours on cold email × 4 × hourly rate) / contacts sent per month

Total cost per contact = tool cost + data cost + time cost

Run this calculation before committing to a setup. Most founders discover their real cost per contact is 3-5x higher than the tool subscription they were comparing against.

The number that matters more than cost per contact

Cost per contact tells you how efficiently you're reaching people. Cost per qualified reply tells you whether your targeting and copy are working. The second number connects directly to pipeline.

Cost per qualified reply = total spend / positive replies received.

At $0.07/contact with a 4% positive reply rate: $1.75 per qualified reply. At $0.40/contact (DIY tools + time) with the same 4% reply rate: $10 per reply. At $8/contact (agency) with a 5% reply rate: $160 per reply.

Two of those conversations converting to paying customers at $2,000 average deal value puts each scenario's ROI at roughly 1,140x, 200x, and 25x respectively. The math doesn't change the outcome, it changes how many conversations you can have per dollar spent.
